Local Voice Lab

How to make speech that doesn't sound like a robot — entirely on your own machine, for free, with nothing about your text ever leaving the box. This is the course behind the narration you already heard: the DSA presentations and the One Piece episodes, where Luffy laughs, Nami dreads the brute-force trap and lights up at the clever answer, and every crew member has their own voice. That was built with a cloud service (edge-tts) that sounds great and quietly ships every line you synthesize to Microsoft's servers. Here you rebuild the whole thing locally — Piper, Coqui XTTS, Kokoro, StyleTTS2, Bark — so the exact same result runs offline, privately, and reproducibly.

Taught like the other courses: real mechanics, a One Piece story that acts each idea out (plus a Big Bang Theory or Friends scene), a "Technicality corner" for the questions you'd be embarrassed to ask, a hands-on Code Lab that runs pure-Python pieces right in the browser, a quiz, and the interview questions companies actually ask. The running product is StrawHat Narrator — a fully-local, character-voiced, emotion-tuned narration renderer you assemble piece by piece.

Every lesson teaches the essentials by default — flip Essentials → Full Depth in the header any time you want a topic's deeper mechanism, edge cases, and "why it's actually built that way." Nothing is gated behind it; it's purely how far a lesson goes.

The models live in your own cache, downloaded only when you choose to; the course site itself stays tiny. All music and sound effects on the site are generated live in your browser with the Web Audio API — original, nothing to license.

How to use this

  1. This is a builder's course. The lessons teach every piece — the engines, the prosody and emotion tricks, the render pipeline, the privacy checks — and you assemble StrawHat Narrator yourself on your own machine as you go.
  2. In each lesson: read the concept, open the story + animation the first time through, expand any Technicality corner question you can't already answer out loud, then do the Code Lab — small pure-Python pieces run right in the browser; model-dependent labs come with an exact "run it locally" recipe instead.
  3. Before marking a lesson complete, try the interview questions at the bottom out loud.
  4. Keep the Cheat Sheet open in a second tab — every engine, install command, and pipeline recipe in one place.
  5. Finish with the timed Interview Drill — randomized questions from everything you studied, on a countdown.
