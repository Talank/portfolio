Built for a working RN with current BLS — not for someone learning CPR for the first time.
Skipping

Compression mechanics, rescue-breathing ratios, AED pad placement, choking response, one-rescuer vs two-rescuer sequencing. That's BLS territory you already hold. This tool starts where BLS ends: rhythm interpretation, drug therapy, electrical therapy, and the algorithms that sequence them under a team.

ACLS is BLS plus four things layered on top: a monitor (so you're treating a specific rhythm, not just "no pulse"), a pharmacy (so you know what to push and when), a defibrillator with more settings than one button (sync vs. unsync, energy selection), and a team you're either running or working inside of. Everything in this tool hangs off one of those four pegs.

How the exam actually tests you

Provider and renewal exams lean on scenario-based multiple choice: you're given a rhythm strip or a clinical vignette and asked for the next correct action, not a definition. The megacode/skills portion tests whether you can run an algorithm out loud under time pressure without freezing on drug math. Both reward pattern recognition over memorized trivia — which is why this tool leans on mnemonics and flashcards instead of walls of text.

Suggested order

  1. Rhythm Recognition — if you can't name the rhythm, the algorithm choice is a guess.
  2. Cardiac Arrest → Bradycardia → Tachycardia — the three algorithms that structure everything else.
  3. Drug Reference + Electrical Therapy — the tools those algorithms call on.
  4. ACS, Stroke, Post-ROSC — the "what happens after/around the arrest" algorithms.
  5. Mnemonic Hub → Cheat Sheet → Practice — compression and self-test in the last 48 hours before your exam.

The Systematic Approach & Team Dynamics

One framework, run in order, every time — it's what keeps a code from becoming noise.

BLS survey (check responsiveness, call for help, C-A-B, defibrillate if indicated) still happens first — you know that part. ACLS adds two more passes: the Primary Assessment and the Secondary Assessment. Run both, in order, every time, even when the answer seems obvious.

Primary — ABCDEWhat you're actually doing
AirwayPatent? Need an advanced airway? If one's placed, confirm and continuously monitor with waveform capnography — not just a color-change device.
BreathingRate, effort, SpO₂, bilateral breath sounds. Avoid excessive ventilation — it raises intrathoracic pressure and drops coronary perfusion pressure during compressions.
CirculationMonitor/leads on, pulse and BP, IV/IO access x2 if possible, identify the rhythm.
DisabilityQuick neuro check — AVPU or GCS, pupils, glucose.
ExposureFully expose, look for the clue you'd otherwise miss — track marks, a med patch, JVD, unequal breath sounds.
Secondary Assessment
SAMPLE hxSigns/symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last oral intake, Events leading up.

Pearl

Waveform capnography is quietly one of the highest-yield ACLS concepts: it confirms tube placement, gauges CPR quality (target ETCO₂ ≥ 10 mmHg, ideally >20), and is often the earliest objective sign of ROSC — a sudden sustained jump in ETCO₂ during compressions, sometimes before you'd feel a pulse.

High-performance team dynamics

AHA names this explicitly because it's a testable, distinct skill — not soft-skill filler.

The six elements

  • Clear roles & responsibilities — everyone knows their job before it starts.
  • Knowing your limitations — ask for help before you're in over your head.
  • Constructive intervention — speak up if you see an error, respectfully and immediately.
  • Knowledge sharing — think out loud; don't sit on a differential.
  • Closed-loop communication — order given → order repeated back → order confirmed done.
  • Clear, direct messages — no ambiguity, no shouted overlap.

Closed-loop, concretely

Leader: "Give epinephrine 1 milligram IV now."
Nurse: "Giving epinephrine 1 milligram IV now."
Nurse, after: "Epinephrine 1 milligram IV given at 14:32."

Three steps, every drug, every shock. It's the single most testable team-dynamics behavior on a megacode station.

From the code cart

The quietest code is usually the best-run one. Closed-loop communication means one voice giving orders, one voice confirming — not everyone calling out the rhythm at once. If you're not the leader and not the one being addressed, you're either doing your task or watching the monitor.

Foundations

Rhythm Recognition — the ECG Lab

Five questions, asked in the same order, on every strip — then a live lab that answers them in front of you, rhythm by rhythm.

The framework — mnemonic: "Rate, Rhythm, P, PR, QRS"

  1. Rate — count it (6-second strip × 10, or 1500 ÷ small boxes between R waves).
  2. Regularity — regular, regularly irregular, or irregularly irregular.
  3. P waves — present? One per QRS? Same shape each time?
  4. PR interval — constant, lengthening, or unrelated to the QRS?
  5. QRS width — narrow (<0.12s, supraventricular origin) or wide (≥0.12s, ventricular origin or aberrant conduction)?

Everything downstream — which algorithm you open, which drug you reach for — falls out of those five answers. Narrow + irregular is a different drug than wide + regular, even though both can look "fast and scary" at first glance.

The ECG Lab

Pick a rhythm. The monitor sweeps it live, and the five framework questions walk you through it — each step drawing its evidence directly on the strip: R-wave counts for rate, calipers for regularity, markers on every P wave, PR brackets, and QRS-width shading. Work left to right through the chips; the blocks are grouped at the end so you can drill them back-to-back against the block poem below.

The block poem — the only heart-block mnemonic you need

"If the R is far from P, then you have a FIRST degree.
Longer, longer, longer, DROP — then you have a WENCKEBACH.
If some Ps don't get through, then you have MOBITZ II.
If Ps and Qs don't agree, then you have a THIRD degree."

Exam shortcut hiding inside it: the first two lines (1st degree, Wenckebach) are the benign, usually atropine-responsive blocks. The last two lines (Mobitz II, 3rd degree) are the dangerous, atropine-refractory ones — the poem's second half is your "prepare to pace" list.

Exam habit

Never name a rhythm from its silhouette alone — the exam deliberately picks lookalikes (flutter 2:1 vs SVT, fine VF vs asystole, Mobitz II vs Wenckebach). Run the five questions in order every single time, even when you "recognize" the strip instantly. The framework is what catches the trap options.

Algorithm

Adult Cardiac Arrest

VF/pVT and PEA/Asystole — one algorithm, two branches, same clock.

Start CPR, attach the monitor/defibrillator, and answer one question: is the rhythm shockable?

Shockable — VF / pulseless VT
  • ShockDefibrillate immediately, unsynchronized.
  • CPR × 2 minResume compressions instantly — no pulse check right after the shock.
  • Rhythm check → still shockable? Shock again.Then resume CPR immediately.
  • Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IOAfter the 2nd shock, then every 3–5 min.
  • Consider advanced airway + capnographyOnce available without stopping compressions.
  • Amiodarone 300 mg (or lidocaine 1–1.5 mg/kg)After the 3rd shock. Second dose: amiodarone 150 mg or lidocaine 0.5–0.75 mg/kg.
  • Treat reversible causes; repeat cycleShock → CPR 2 min → rhythm check, drug every other cycle.
Non-shockable — PEA / Asystole
  • CPR × 2 minNo shock indicated.
  • Epinephrine 1 mg IV/IO ASAPGive as soon as feasible, then every 3–5 min.
  • Rhythm/pulse check every 2 minRhythm becomes shockable? Switch branches.
  • Consider advanced airway + capnographySame threshold as the shockable arm.
  • Hunt and treat H's & T's aggressivelyPEA/asystole survival hinges on finding the reversible cause — antiarrhythmics don't apply here.
  • Repeat cycleCPR 2 min → epi q3–5min → reassess.
2025 update

Epinephrine timing now differs by branch: shockable rhythms get their first dose after the 2nd shock; non-shockable rhythms get it as soon as feasible. Vasopressin is not recommended as a substitute for or addition to epinephrine — there's no outcome benefit. Double sequential defibrillation (two defibrillators, near-simultaneous shocks) carries a Class IIb "may be considered" for refractory VF after 3 standard shocks fail — it is not yet a routine first-line step.

Related

Opioid-associated life-threatening emergency: assess responsiveness/breathing → call for help → if not breathing normally, start ventilations/CPR as indicated → give naloxone (0.4–2 mg IV/IM/IN, may repeat) as soon as available without interrupting resuscitation. If a non-opioid arrest, naloxone does no harm — when uncertain, give it anyway and keep ventilating.

From the code cart

Push epinephrine right after compressions resume, not before — chasing the rhythm-check pause to give the drug is what actually delays the compressions generating coronary perfusion pressure. And confirm sync is off before you defibrillate; sync mode is for cardioversion, not for VF.

Algorithm

Bradycardia With a Pulse

HR < 50 becomes a problem only when it's causing symptoms.

Identify and treat the underlying cause first. Monitor, O₂ if hypoxic, IV access, 12-lead if it doesn't delay therapy. The branch point is whether bradycardia is causing hypotension, acutely altered mental status, signs of shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure.

  • Persistent bradyarrhythmia causing symptoms?If no — monitor and observe.
  • Atropine 1 mg IVRepeat every 3–5 min, max total 3 mg.
  • Atropine ineffective, or unlikely to workHigh-grade block (Mobitz II, 3rd-degree with wide QRS) often won't respond — don't wait on repeat doses.
  • Transcutaneous pacingAnd/or dopamine infusion 5–20 mcg/kg/min, and/or epinephrine infusion 2–10 mcg/min.
  • Consider expert consult + transvenous pacingEspecially if pacing/pressors aren't holding.
From the code cart

Full atropinization needs the full 3 mg. A single 1 mg dose in a high-degree block often just buys a few minutes of false reassurance — don't be surprised when you're setting up for pacing anyway. Also: transcutaneous pacing hurts. If the patient is conscious, sedate/analgese before you crank the current up.

Mnemonic

"1, up to 3, then Pace or Push." Atropine 1 mg doses, up to 3 mg max; beyond that, pace (transcutaneous) or push pressors (dopamine/epi infusion).

Algorithm

Tachycardia With a Pulse

Unstable or stable first. Narrow or wide, regular or irregular, second.
Unstable
  • Hypotension, AMS, shock, ischemic pain, or acute HF caused by the tachycardia
  • Immediate synchronized cardioversionSedate if time allows — don't delay if truly unstable.
  • Exception: irregular wide-complex (polymorphic VT/torsades)Treat as VF — unsynchronized defibrillation, not cardioversion.
Stable — 12-lead, then sort by width & regularity
  • Narrow & regularVagal maneuvers → adenosine 6 mg rapid IVP+flush → 12 mg if needed.
  • Narrow & irregularLikely AFib/AFlutter/MAT — rate control (β-blocker/CCB), avoid adenosine, consider anticoagulation.
  • Wide & regularTreat as VT until proven otherwise. Antiarrhythmic infusion (procainamide, amiodarone, or sotalol) — expert consult.
  • Wide & irregularPossible pre-excited AFib (WPW) — avoid AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, β-blocker, CCB, digoxin); expert consult.
From the code cart

Wide and weird, with a history of structural heart disease? It usually is VT — treat it that way rather than reaching for a nodal blocker on a hunch. And always warn the patient before adenosine: "you're going to feel your heart pause for a second, that's expected." Skip the warning and you'll have a very alarmed patient — and family — at bedside.

Mnemonic

"Sync what's organized, shock what's chaos." Any organized QRS you're cardioverting gets sync mode. VF and irregular wide-complex (no reliable QRS to sync to) get unsynchronized defibrillation.

Algorithm

Post-Cardiac Arrest Care

ROSC isn't the finish line — the next 20 minutes re-arrest more patients than the original event.

The bundle — mnemonic: "VITALS"

  • Ventilation — titrate to SpO₂ 92–98%, normocapnia; avoid hyperventilation.
  • Investigate the cause — 12-lead ECG, echo, consider emergent cath if STEMI/high suspicion.
  • Temperature control — 32–37.5°C for ≥24 hours in patients unresponsive to commands; treat any fever >37.5°C aggressively.
  • Arterial pressure — MAP ≥ 65 mmHg; fluids/vasopressors as needed.
  • Level of glucose — monitor and control.
  • Seizure watch — continuous EEG in comatose patients; treat seizures.
2025 update

Temperature target widened from the old 32–36°C to 32–37.5°C, maintained ≥24 h (reasonable up to 36 h). Fever is actively avoided, but strict hypothermia is no longer mandated. Neuroprognostication is delayed at least 72 hours post-arrest and post-rewarming — no single exam finding or test is used in isolation.

From the code cart

The first 20 minutes after ROSC are deceptively dangerous. Patients re-arrest most often here from unrecognized hypotension or hypoxia — not from the original cause repeating itself. Don't let the room relax just because there's a pulse.

Algorithm

Acute Coronary Syndromes

MONA is retired. The order and the conditions attached to each piece now matter.

Recognize early — chest discomfort ± radiation, dyspnea, diaphoresis, and remember atypical presentation is common in women, older adults, and people with diabetes. Get a 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of first contact; don't let anything delay it.

ANOM — MONA, reordered and rationed

  • Aspirin 160–325 mg, chewed, non-enteric, first and fast (unless a true contraindication).
  • Nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL, may repeat q5min ×3 — hold if SBP < 90, suspected RV infarct, or a PDE5-inhibitor (sildenafil-class) taken in the last 24–48h.
  • Oxygen only if SpO₂ < 90% or respiratory distress — routine high-flow O₂ in a non-hypoxic patient doesn't help and may worsen infarct size.
  • Morphine last, only for pain refractory to nitrates — used judiciously, not routinely.

More oxygen is not automatically better here. A high-flow mask on a patient sitting at 96% doesn't improve outcomes and may increase infarct size — reach for it only when the number actually says hypoxic.

Algorithm

Suspected Stroke

Time is brain — quite literally, the treatment window is the whole algorithm.

Recognition — BE-FAST

  • Balance — sudden loss of balance or coordination.
  • Eyes — sudden vision loss or double vision.
  • Face — facial droop, ask them to smile.
  • Arm — arm drift or weakness, ask them to raise both.
  • Speech — slurred or garbled speech, ask them to repeat a phrase.
  • Time — note exactly when the patient was last known well; it drives every downstream decision.

Time targets — mnemonic: "20-60-3"

  • CT (or MRI) read within 20 minutes of ED arrival.
  • Door-to-needle (thrombolytic) within 60 minutes, ideally 30–45.
  • Admit to a stroke unit within 3 hours of arrival.
  • Fibrinolytic (tenecteplase or alteplase) within 4.5 hours of last known well, if no contraindications (anticoagulation, recent surgery/bleed, uncontrolled BP).
  • Mechanical thrombectomy for large-vessel occlusion up to 24 hours from last known well in selected patients.
  • BP: permissive hypertension unless giving a fibrinolytic, then keep < 185/110 before and during treatment.
  • Check glucose immediately — hypoglycemia is a classic stroke mimic.
From the code cart

Ask about anticoagulant use and pin down last-known-well time before the patient even leaves the waiting room. Those two facts alone drive almost every decision that follows.

From the code cart

Confirm the sync markers are actually landing on the R wave before you shock in sync mode — a sync failure that fires on a T wave can cause R-on-T and precipitate the exact rhythm (VF) you were trying to avoid. And after any shock, resume compressions immediately; don't pause to feel for a pulse first.

Pad placement pearls

  • Anterior-lateral is the default; anterior-posterior is the alternative (sometimes preferred for AFib cardioversion).
  • Keep pads > 8 cm from an implanted pacemaker/ICD generator.
  • Remove nitro patches and excess chest hair issues before placement; dry the chest if diaphoretic.
  • For pacing, confirm both electrical capture (spike + wide QRS) and mechanical capture (a palpable pulse) — electrical capture alone isn't enough.

H's & T's — Reversible Causes

Run the full list out loud in every PEA/asystole arrest. It's what catches the one you'd otherwise miss.

The 5 H's

  • Hypovolemia — flat neck veins, history of bleeding/fluid loss → fluids, blood.
  • Hypoxia — check airway, ventilation, SpO₂ → 100% O₂, secure airway.
  • Hydrogen ion (acidosis) — known renal failure, prolonged arrest → ventilation, treat cause; bicarb only if specifically indicated.
  • Hypo-/Hyperkalemia — dialysis patient, peaked or flattened T waves → see treatment below.
  • Hypothermia — cold exposure history, low core temp → active rewarming.

The 5 T's

  • Tension pneumothorax — absent breath sounds, JVD, tracheal deviation → needle decompression.
  • Tamponade, cardiac — JVD, muffled heart sounds, history of trauma/effusion → pericardiocentesis.
  • Toxins — overdose history, drug vials at scene → specific antidote (e.g., naloxone).
  • Thrombosis, pulmonary — sudden collapse, recent surgery/immobility → consider fibrinolysis.
  • Thrombosis, coronary — cardiac history, prior ECG changes → cath lab/fibrinolysis.

Hyperkalemia treatment — mnemonic: "C BIG K Drop"

  • Calcium chloride/gluconate — stabilizes the cardiac membrane first.
  • Bicarbonate — shifts K⁺ intracellularly.
  • Insulin (with Glucose) — drives K⁺ intracellularly.
  • Kayexalate / diuretics / Dialysis — actually removes potassium from the body.
From the code cart

Say the whole list out loud during a code, even when it feels redundant — verbalizing it is what catches the tension pneumothorax you'd otherwise miss at minute 14, once everyone's attention has narrowed to the monitor.
